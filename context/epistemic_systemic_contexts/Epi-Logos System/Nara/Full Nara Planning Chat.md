Research: Dialogical and Inquiry-Based Practices: Three Traditions

David Bohm’s Dialogue (Bohmian Dialogue)

Philosophical Foundations: Physicist David Bohm developed dialogue as a response to what he saw as pervasive fragmentation in human thought and society. Bohm’s worldview was rooted in the idea of an underlying wholeness and the “implicate order” – a deeper, interconnected reality from which our visible (“explicate”) world unfolds. In conversation, this translates to the notion that beneath our differing opinions lies a shared field of meaning waiting to emerge. Bohm believed that most communication is incoherent and riddled with assumptions, which leads to conflict. By becoming aware of the process of thought (not just its content) and suspending our assumptions, a group can allow a free flow of meaning to unfold collectively. This free-flowing, participatory thought is aimed at overcoming fragmentation and tapping into a deeper coherence and creativity in thinking together. Bohm often emphasized that “shared meaning” is the cement of social reality – when that meaning is poor or fragmented, society breaks down. Dialogue, in his view, is a way to repair this by returning our awareness to the whole of thought and its impacts.

Group Protocols and Key Principles: A Bohm dialogue is typically conducted in groups (Bohm suggested ~20 to 40 people) seated in a circle, with no formal agenda or preset topic. The atmosphere is informal yet attentive, often lasting for several hours at a time so that conversation can unfold organically. Crucially, no group decision or action is to be made during the dialogue – it is “an empty space” free from goals or conclusions. There is also no official leader; if a facilitator is present to get things started, their role is to step back once the dialogue begins. These conditions create a safe container in which participants are not pressured to persuade or perform, but to observe and learn from the process of thought itself. Bohm distinguished dialogue from discussion or debate: in a typical discussion, people hold fast to positions and aim to win arguments, whereas in dialogue everyone wins by gaining insight – it’s a cooperative exploration rather than a competition.

Core Practices (Suspension and Listening): Participants in Bohmian dialogue agree to a set of simple yet profound practices that enable genuine communication. Some foundational guidelines include:

No decisions or agendas: The group does not convene to solve a problem or reach consensus on action. This creates a free space where no one is obligated to achieve any outcome.

Suspend assumptions and judgments: Everyone is encouraged to “suspend” their assumptions – meaning neither act on them nor suppress them, but instead hold them lightly in front of oneself. If someone hears an idea they dislike, they refrain from attacking or immediately refuting it. All opinions are observed as contributions to the group thought process, rather than truths to defend or reject.

Speak authentically and transparently: While suspending judgment, participants are also as honest as possible. One should not withhold a “hot” or controversial thought out of fear; instead, voice it without hostility. The dialogue works when people put forward their true opinions and feelings (as opinions, not absolute facts) in a spirit of trust.

Deep listening and shared meaning: Participants listen to each other without interruption and without “reloading” their own arguments. They attempt to build on each other’s ideas or at least consider them, rather than debating. The aim is that by hearing differences, the group as a whole can perceive new facets of the issue. In Bohm’s words, people in dialogue are “not attempting to make common certain ideas… that are already known” but “creating something new together” – a shared meaning that emerges from the interplay of perspectives. This creative, generative aspect is the hallmark of dialogue.


“Proprioception” of Thought: Underlying these practices is Bohm’s call for a proprioception (self-awareness) of thought. Just as the body has proprioception (sense of itself), Bohm suggested that individuals and the group need to observe the process of thinking in real time. By noticing how one’s own reactions (e.g. a surge of anger or a defensive impulse) arise in the conversation, participants can suspend those reactions and thereby prevent the dialogue from derailing into argument. This shared mindfulness of thought helps expose hidden tacit beliefs and cultural assumptions that usually distort communication. Through dialogue, the group may become aware of collective thought patterns and incoherences (such as “us vs. them” framings or contradictory demands we each carry) and possibly dissolve them. The result, ideally, is a greater coherence in thinking – a unified understanding that no single person could have reached alone. Participants often report a shift from defensive postures to a sense of fellowship and meaningful connection in the group.

One-on-One Adaptation: Bohm envisioned dialogue mainly in group settings to mimic a microcosm of society, but he acknowledged that the spirit of dialogue can also apply in smaller settings or even internally. He noted that “this spirit of the dialogue can work even in smaller groups, or one-on-one, or within the individual”, so long as the same attitudes are maintained. In a one-on-one conversation, this means both people would commit to the dialogical principles: no agenda or debate, mutual suspension of judgment, and truly listening to understand each other’s meaning. With practice, two people can thus engage in a Bohmian dialogue, essentially by creating that “free space” between them for truth to emerge. Likewise, an individual journaling or reflecting can try to hold all sides of an inner conflict in mind without jumping to conclusions – effectively a dialogue with oneself. The key is the attitude of openness and inquiry. Indeed, Bohm suggested that if an individual can “hold all of the meanings together in his own mind,” then he or she has the attitude of dialogue and can communicate it to others. In summary, while a circle of ~20 people was Bohm’s ideal format to stir rich diversity and even conflict (so that hidden assumptions surface), the essence of Bohm’s dialogue – thoughtful, collective inquiry – can scale down to two people or one person. The crucial elements remain the same: suspend certainty, listen deeply, and allow shared meaning to unfold fresh.

Native American Talking Circles (Glenn Aparicio Parry and the SEED Institute)

Indigenous Origins and Philosophy: The talking circle is a traditional dialogical practice found in many Native American cultures (with notable roots in the Plains tribes) that emphasizes inclusive, sacred communication. Its origins trace back to the oldest human tradition of gathering around a fire to share stories and resolve issues collectively. In indigenous worldview, speech is not just informational but ceremonial and powerful – words are seen as carrying spirit and intent. Therefore, speaking and listening in a circle are done with reverence. Glenn Aparicio Parry, a scholar and founder of the SEED Institute, has worked extensively with Native elders (such as Leroy Little Bear of the Blackfoot people) to highlight the principles of the talking circle and integrate them with modern dialogue practices. Parry explains that in many Indigenous traditions a tribal consciousness prevails: individuals feel themselves to be part of an interconnected whole (people, nature, and spirit). This means that true dialogue is less about individual opinions and more about tuning into a collective mind or the “thought waves of nature”. In the words of Elder Little Bear, “The Native view... it’s all wave” – reality is a flowing holistic process rather than fragmented bits. Thus, the talking circle operates on an underlying spiritual premise: if participants purify their hearts and listen deeply, a higher or communal truth will manifest through the circle.

Structure and Protocols: A Native American talking circle has a well-defined yet simple structure that embodies equality, respect, and patience. While specific customs can vary by tribe, the general format is as follows:

Sacred circle format: Participants sit in a circle, often with no table or barrier, so that everyone is face-to-face in an unbroken shape (symbolizing the wholeness of the community). In some traditions the circle is literally “opened” toward the Eastern direction as a sign of welcome to spiritual guidance (the East, where the sun rises, can represent illumination). There is no hierarchy in seating; the circle itself is the “leader.” When gathered, the circle may begin with a moment of silence or a prayer to sanctify the space.

Use of a Talking Stick: To ensure orderly and heartfelt communication, the group uses a talking object – often a talking stick, staff, or sacred feather. This object is passed around the circle *clockwise (sunwise)*. Only the person holding the talking stick has the floor to speak, and everyone else remains silent, listening attentively. The speaker may speak for as long as needed; there is no rigid time limit. The talking stick itself is imbued with significance: it is said to carry the “connection to the spirit world of thought and energy”, lending authority and presence to the speaker’s words. When finished, the speaker passes the stick to the next person in the circle, who then has their turn. This ritual ensures each voice is heard fully without interruption or crosstalk.

Ceremonial opening and mindset: A talking circle is often preceded by a ceremonial opening to cultivate the right mindset. For example, participants might engage in a smudging ritual (burning sage or cedar) to symbolically cleanse negative energy, or even a short prayer or song, inviting guidance from the Great Spirit. In some cases, a sweat lodge or other purification rite might be conducted beforehand. The underlying idea is to help everyone **“come to the dialogue with a purified mind and heart”** – letting go of ego agendas, anger, or preconceived ideas. In the circle, each person strives to be fully present and genuine, acting as a conduit for Spirit or truth to speak through them. This spiritual underpinning distinguishes the talking circle from a mere discussion; the process is held as a sacred trust.


Principles of Engagement: Within this structure, there are clear rules of engagement that all participants honor, ensuring the space remains safe and respectful:

Speak from the heart: When holding the talking stick, one is encouraged to speak honestly, truthfully, and from one’s deepest feelings or experience. It’s not a time for abstract arguments or grandstanding, but for authentic sharing. This could mean telling a personal story related to the issue at hand, voicing a concern, or offering a prayer. There’s an understanding that words spoken from the heart carry spiritual weight.

Listen with respect (and without interruption): Listening is as important as speaking in a talking circle – arguably more so. While one person speaks, everyone else practices deep listening, not only to the words but also to the spirit behind the words. As Parry notes, listening is considered part of the speaking: both speaker and listeners “clear their minds” to receive whatever message is coming through the speaker. No one may interrupt or interject while another has the talking stick. This requires patience and an ability to sit with silence; often there are pauses as a person gathers their thoughts or emotions, and that silence is respected. By listening in this open way, participants attempt to hear beyond their own filters, perhaps catching the deeper meaning or feeling that the speaker conveys.

No direct rebuttal or debate: The circle is not a forum for back-and-forth debate. Even if someone disagrees with a point made, they do not verbally object in that moment. Instead, each person, when their turn comes, can share their perspective in turn, but always addressing the whole circle, not arguing at a specific individual. This creates a field of many perspectives without anyone getting into a personal duel. In traditional circles, if the meeting is convened around a particular person’s problem or question, that person might speak first to state their concern, then remain mostly silent thereafter. Others in the circle will offer their viewpoints, stories, or advice one by one. Importantly, they do not tell the person with the concern what to do or attempt to solve the problem directly. They simply share what they see or know, as an offering. The person with the issue listens to all the contributions without responding immediately. This non-confrontational approach ensures that wisdom is offered freely, without anyone feeling pressured or criticized.

Confidentiality and trust: Although not explicitly mentioned in Parry’s excerpt, it is generally understood in such circles that what is shared is personal and often sensitive. Participants are expected to treat the sharing as confidential (or “sacred”) – meaning they don’t gossip about it later or use someone’s words against them. This norm builds trust, allowing people to speak more freely from the heart.

Equality and courtesy: Each person in the circle has an equal right to speak. Elders or leaders, if present, may have a guiding role (and are deeply respected), but they do not dominate the discussion. In some cases, an elder or facilitator might gently remind the group of the guidelines if someone goes off track. Overall, everyone’s voice – whether shy or bold, young or old – carries equal weight in the sacred space of the circle. This egalitarian spirit reflects the indigenous belief that every individual has a piece of the truth and something valuable to contribute.


Emergence of Shared Understanding (Consensus): The goal of a talking circle is not to vote on a decision but to arrive at a shared understanding or consensus in the true sense of the word. Here consensus doesn’t mean everyone explicitly agrees on a course of action; rather, it refers to an unspoken collective alignment.
